# Anchor — Ideal Customer Profile

---

## Persona: The Moment Loser

*Someone who lives richly but can't hold onto it.*

They are not defined by a job title or demographic bracket. They are defined by a specific feeling: the awareness that things they care about are slipping through. A conversation that mattered. A period of life that already feels blurry. The version of themselves from two years ago that they can barely access.

They may have tried journaling before and stopped, or never started because no approach ever felt quite right. Either way, they have fragments: a half-filled notebook, a voice memos folder they never revisit, a Notes app with undated dumps. The intention was always there. The system never held — or never existed in the first place.

---

## Who they are

**Persona name:** The Moment Loser

**Life stage:** Mid-20s to mid-40s. In a phase of life that feels significant — building something, navigating something, changing. Aware that they won't remember it the way they're living it.

**Relationship to journaling:** Either tried it and stopped, or never found an approach that fit. In both cases, the desire was real — what failed was the system. The friction was always slightly higher than the motivation on any given day, or the blank page never felt like the right format for how they think.

**Relationship to technology:** Comfortable enough. Uses voice memos naturally, has a notes system of some kind even if chaotic. Not interested in configuring tools — wants things that work immediately.

---

## What they need to do (Job-to-be-Done)

> *When I experience something I want to remember, I need to capture it before it fades — and trust that I'll be able to find and re-enter that moment later.*

The job is not "to journal." It is to preserve specific, lived experience in a form that lets them reconstruct it — not just know it happened. The difference between reading a summary and feeling like they're back there.

This matters most in three moments:
1. **Right after something happens** — a conversation, a realisation, an experience they know they'll want later
2. **At the end of a difficult or meaningful period** — when they want to look back and see what they were actually going through
3. **When they're trying to remember something specific** — and can't find it or aren't sure it was written down

---

## Why they need it now (Acute pain / urgency)

The pain is not acute in a dramatic sense — it's low-grade and accumulating. It shows up as:

- Looking back at last year and realising they can barely account for it
- Knowing a relationship or period of life was important but not being able to access the detail
- Finding a voice memo from 6 months ago and realising they have no system for what to do with it
- Starting a new journal — again — and feeling the familiar dread that it won't stick

The urgency is existential rather than operational: *I am living my life and I am losing it at the same time.*

Anchor doesn't solve a workflow problem. It solves a memory problem.

---

## How they behave (Usage patterns)

**Capture frequency:** Multiple times a week, sometimes daily during heightened periods. Rarely during flat stretches.

**Capture context:** In-the-moment, not in dedicated sessions. Walking, commuting, right after something happened, before bed. The session is rarely longer than 5 minutes.

**Preferred input mode:** Voice when alone and mobile. Typing when in a public or quiet context. Photo upload when the note was handwritten. They move between modes without thinking about it.

**Review frequency:** Monthly synthesis is aspirational but not currently happening — because it requires manual effort they don't make time for. Would engage with it if it arrived without them having to trigger it.

**Failure mode:** Inconsistent capture during low-energy periods. Large gaps that feel embarrassing to return from. This is the most common reason existing systems break down.

---

## What they value (The immediate win)

**The immediate win:** Something gets captured in under a minute, is automatically organised, and they feel confident it's there when they need it.

**The medium-term win:** Monthly synthesis surfaces a moment they'd half-forgotten. They feel like they're being seen by their own archive.

**The long-term win:** Looking back at a year and actually remembering it — not as a blur, but as specific scenes with texture. A record that does the work their memory couldn't.

What they do *not* primarily value:
- The format (markdown vs. anything else)
- Data ownership as a concept — they care that their data is safe and accessible, but "you own your files" is not what sells them on a product
- Features they don't immediately use

---

## Psychographics

**Motivated by:** The fear of losing their own story. Desire for self-continuity. Curiosity about patterns in their own life.

**Resistant to:** Setup friction. Tools that feel like a second job. Anything that makes them feel like they've failed if they miss a day.

**Self-perception:** They see themselves as someone who wants to journal, or has always meant to. They don't see themselves as bad at it — they see the format as having always been slightly wrong for how they actually live.

**Language they use:** "I keep meaning to," "I used to journal," "I have voice memos I've never listened to," "I wish I'd written that down," "I just never found something that stuck."

---

## What Anchor is *not* for

- People who want to process emotions in a structured therapeutic format (that's a different product)
- Productivity journalers tracking goals and habits (closer to Notion or Roam)
- Writers who journal as craft practice
- People who are already consistent journalers and have a system that works
